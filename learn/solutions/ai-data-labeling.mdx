If you buy human labels or run an RLHF workforce, you are paying for the judgment of distinct real people. [HumanCheck](/learn/where-humancheck-fits) binds each labeler to one verified, live human, so a single person cannot run a farm of accounts and a script cannot pose as a worker.

## The problem

Label quality rests on one quiet assumption: that each worker is a separate, real person actually doing the work. When that assumption breaks, everything downstream breaks with it.

- One person opens dozens of labeler accounts to collect dozens of payouts for the same work.
- A script submits labels no human ever looked at.
- Sybil workers pad your headcount without adding a single independent judgment.

The throughput numbers look healthy the whole time. Underneath, your dataset fills with correlated, low-diversity, sometimes machine-generated labels, and a model that was supposed to learn from many independent humans is quietly learning from a handful of gamed accounts.

It costs you twice. You pay for labels that are not from distinct humans, and then you pay again to find and scrub the contamination out of a training set, if you catch it at all. One person running fifty accounts is fifty payouts for one person's work and fifty seats' worth of judgment that all trace back to the same head.

## How HumanCheck helps

Two things do the work. **Liveness** proves a real, live human is present at the moment of the check, not a recording, not a generated face, not a script, so a bot cannot stand in for a worker. **Uniqueness** proves that human is not already here under another account, so one person cannot run many labeler identities. Together they bind each seat to exactly one real person.

Because your labelers have accounts, this is a member-shaped use case. You verify the human once, tie that verified human to their labeler identity, and recognize them when they come back for their next session. If the same face later turns up trying to open a second account under a new email or phone, the check catches it instead of minting another seat.

## A configuration that fits

A good starting point for a labeling or RLHF workforce, using the real settings on every check. Treat this as a first draft your VerifyYou contact tunes with you, not a fixed recipe.

| Setting | Starting point | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Identity mode | signup or provided | Labelers become members. Use **signup** if the check is their front door, or **provided** if your platform already knows their email or phone and you pass it in. |
| Identity lock | on | Binds one face to one labeler identity. If that face reappears under a different email or phone, the check returns an `identity_mismatch`, which is exactly the farmed-account move you want to stop. |
| Collision scope | network | Looks across all of VerifyYou, not just your own platform, so someone who has been farming labeler accounts elsewhere is known to you on day one. |
| Collision action | block | Turns a duplicate away outright. In labeling a repeat account is rarely a false alarm worth waiting on. |
| Skip re-verify | time-windowed | A labeler re-proves at the start of a session rather than scanning before every task. Low friction, without letting a seat stay open forever on one old proof. |

<Note>
  Skip re-verify changes what a returning labeler feels, not what the check records. A returning person who skips the scan still counts as a verification for billing. [Configuring your verification](/learn/configuring-your-verification) covers every setting in full.
</Note>
