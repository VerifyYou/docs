## The problem

Trust breaks at scale. The moment something is worth gaming, a signup, a survey, a payout, a gated offer, people find a way: bots, fake accounts, one person quietly running fifteen identities, bulk buyers. The usual fix is to insert a full identity check, but you cannot solve trust by inserting an identity. You need a layer in between zero and identity. Most teams do not need to know who someone is. They need to know the person is real, and that they are only here once.

## The economics of fraud

Fraud is an economic decision. Someone games a signup, a survey, or a payout when faking a presence costs less than what that presence is worth. For most of the internet's life that kept fraud in check, because passing as a real human took real effort. AI has erased that effort, and the cost of spinning up something that looks and behaves like a person is falling toward zero, so the math tilts further in the attacker's favor every year, and it will keep tilting as the models get better and cheaper. HumanCheck changes it at the point of entry. When every actor has to be a unique, verified human before they get anything, the cost of creating a fake presence climbs above what it could ever earn, and the rational basis for fraud collapses. You stop cleaning up bad actors after the fact and simply make them uneconomic to create.

## What HumanCheck is

HumanCheck is a quick face scan a person does on their phone. No documents, no government ID, no wallet, just their face, in seconds. From that one scan it proves two things.

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Liveness" icon="user" iconType="light">
    A real person is present right now, so you cannot run a bot script against it. Not a photo, not a recording, not an AI generated face.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Uniqueness" icon="fingerprint" iconType="light">
    One human, one account. So one person cannot quietly become fifteen, and when you block someone, it makes it very hard for them to come back under a new name.
  </Card>

Uniqueness is the part most teams are really after. They do not want your users' identities, and they do not want to hold the PII. They want to know that when they block someone, that person cannot come straight back. Your systems only ever receive a verdict, never the face. The selfie is turned into a code, not a saved image.

## How it works

A check is a short, hosted flow. The person does a quick face scan, you get a result your server can trust, and from then on you decide how we make sure it is the same person each time they come back.

<Steps>
  <Step title="The person does a face scan">
    A few seconds on our hosted page confirm a live, present human. Their face is turned into a face vector, a numerical representation of the face's geometry, not a saved image, and that is what lets us recognize a unique person without ever holding a photo.
  </Step>
  <Step title="You get the result">
    Your server exchanges a token for a clear verified or failed result, and you gate access on that. You never receive a face. See [how the check works](/learn/how-it-works) for the full flow.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The person is re-verified when they return">
    This is where it clicks. You decide how we make sure it is the same person each time they come back. Require a fresh check on every visit for the highest assurance, or recognize them quietly in the background via a browser identifier, with no scan at all. Note that clearing cookies or switching browsers ends device-level recognition. Either way, you get a real level of assurance that the person returning is the one you verified.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The trust layer between no checks and KYC

At one end you have no checks, where anyone can sign up as many times as they like. At the other you have full KYC, a government ID, a lot of personal data, real cost, and a user who has to stop and scan a document. HumanCheck is the layer in between, the one most platforms were missing. You drop it into your product without the cost, the PII, or the friction of an ID check, and still get a real layer of trust, because we do not need to know who someone is, only that they are a real, unique human.

Most teams reach for one of two tools and find it does not fit. Passive bot signals guess at risk but never prove a person, so a determined human still runs fifteen accounts. KYC proves a legal identity but brings cost, data, and friction you did not want. HumanCheck sits in the gap: it proves a real, unique, live human without proving who they are.

| | HumanCheck | Passive bot signals | KYC / ID check |
|---|---|---|---|
| What it proves | A real, unique, live human | A probability you are not a bot | A specific legal identity |
| Needs who you are | No, no PII | No | Yes, full identity and documents |
| Blocks one person making many accounts | Yes, uniqueness is the point | No, it only scores risk | Yes, but heavily |
| Cost per check | About one to two cents | Low | Often one to two dollars or more |
| Friction for a real user | Seconds, a face scan, no documents | None, it runs in the background | Minutes, documents and data entry |
| What you hold afterward | A verdict and a stable id | A risk score | Documents and PII |
| Best for | The trust layer between nothing and KYC | A first-pass filter on obvious bots | Regulated identity, like banking |

The three are not rivals so much as different jobs. Passive signals are a cheap first filter. KYC is for when the law makes you know exactly who someone is. HumanCheck is for the much larger middle, where you need a real, unique human and nothing more. See [where it fits](/learn/where-humancheck-fits) for the longer walk-through, including two-factor.

## Questions teams ask

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Will a face check hurt my conversion?">
    A check turns some traffic away, and most of what it turns away is the traffic you did not want. Real users clear it in seconds, a returning person is recognized without doing it again, and you tune how strict it is by use case. See [security and friction](/learn/security-and-friction).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="We already have a bot check. Isn't that enough?">
    A bot check is cheap and worth keeping, but passive signals are easy to fake, and AI is now better at looking human than many people are. A face scan is a much stronger signal, which is why teams come to us after the passive approach stops holding up. HumanCheck sits behind your bot check, for the abuse that now looks human.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Is our users' data safe?">
    Your systems only ever receive a verdict, never a face. The selfie is turned into a one-way face vector, a numerical representation of the face's geometry, not a photo, and we do not store images. The face vector is treated as sensitive biometric data and is what lets us recognize a unique person. See [what we store](/learn/what-we-store) and [security](/security).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="What does it cost?">
    About one to two cents per verification, all in, whether you run the full check or just liveness and uniqueness, and adding age, gender, or geo costs nothing extra. You are billed monthly on usage and charged once per human, so returning people never count again. That is often fifty to a hundred times less than a full KYC check. For current pricing, [talk to us](https://verifyyou.com/demo).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Can we see it work before we commit?">
