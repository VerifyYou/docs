Trust breaks at scale. The moment something is worth gaming, a signup, a survey, a payout, a gated offer, people find a way: bots, fake accounts, one person quietly running fifteen identities, bulk buyers. The usual fix is to insert a full identity check, but that is heavy, expensive, and most teams do not actually need to know who someone is. They need to know the person is real, and that they are only here once. [HumanCheck](/learn/why-humancheck) is the trust layer between nothing and KYC: a real, unique, live human, without the cost, the PII liability, or the friction of an ID check.

This page is for the person deciding where a human check belongs in their stack, and whether to buy it or build it. It sets HumanCheck next to the tools you already run, shows when it is the right tool, and gives you the honest split on buy versus build.

## The layer between nothing and KYC

At one end you have no checks, where anyone can sign up as many times as they like. At the other you have full KYC, where everyone scans a government ID and hands over a pile of personal data. HumanCheck is the step in the middle, and for most platforms it is the step that was missing.

| | No checks | Bot check | HumanCheck | Full KYC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What it proves | nothing | probably not a bot | a real, unique, live human | a named legal identity |
| Stops one person making many accounts | no | no | yes | yes |
| Resists bots, recordings, and AI generated faces | no | weakly | yes, by design | varies |
| Government ID or documents | none | none | none | required |
| Personal data you hold | none | none | none by default | a lot |
| Friction for a real person | none | low | a few seconds, once | high |
| Cost per check | none | low | about one to two cents | often fifty to a hundred times more |

_Note: optional phone or email steps, if enabled, mean VerifyYou holds those identifiers. Your systems still receive only a verdict and a token._

The two ends are not really competitors. No checks is fine until something is worth gaming. Full KYC is the right call when the law makes you confirm a legal identity. The gap between them is where most abuse actually lives, and that gap is the part HumanCheck fills.

<Note>
  HumanCheck does not replace your bot check or your KYC step. It sits between them, for the abuse that gets past a bot check but does not need a full identity to stop.
</Note>

## It is not KYC, and that is the point

KYC is the heavy option. It asks for a government ID, holds a lot of personal data, costs real money, and makes the user stop to pull out their wallet and scan a document. HumanCheck gives you a real layer of trust without any of that, because we do not need to know who someone is. We only need to confirm they are a real, unique human.

The thing most teams are actually after is uniqueness. They do not want your users' identities, and they do not want PII on their side. They want to know that when they block someone, it makes it very hard for that person to come back under a new account. That is what a human check gives you, and for most platforms it is most of the value.

## It is not a bot check either

A bot check is cheap and near zero friction, and it still earns its place against scripts and bulk sign-ups. But passive signals are easy to fake, and AI is now better at looking human than many people are. A face check is a much stronger signal, which is why teams come to us after the passive approach stops holding up. HumanCheck sits behind your bot check, for the abuse that now looks human.

## Two factor, and more

HumanCheck can include a second factor: the optional phone step. The difference is what it is built around. Two factor on its own only proves someone holds a credential, like a phone or a passkey, and one person can hold ten, so it says nothing about whether a real, unique human is there or whether the same person is already behind ten accounts. HumanCheck proves the human first, and adds the phone step as a second factor when you want it. Most teams keep two factor on login and add HumanCheck at signup.

## Buy versus build

Calling a face match API is the easy part. What is hard, and never finished, is everything around it. Here is the honest split.

| | If you build it yourself | With HumanCheck |
|---|---|---|
| Liveness and anti-fake | You own a model that has to keep pace with new attacks, forever | Kept current for you as attacks change |
| Biometric data liability | You hold and secure sensitive biometric data, with the regulatory exposure that brings | We hold the face vector; your systems only ever see a verdict and a stable id |
| Uniqueness across customers | Only the people you have seen yourself, so a fresh account always looks new | One cross-customer network, so someone flagged elsewhere can be known on day one |
| The return moment | You build and maintain your own way to recognize a returning person | Require a fresh check or recognize them quietly from their device, your choice |
| Time to first check | Weeks of build, then ongoing maintenance | A call from the browser or your server |
| Cost | Engineering time, model upkeep, and the security burden, indefinitely | About one to two cents per verification, charged once per human, billed on usage |

The pattern is the same as anywhere else in your stack. You could build your own payments, your own email delivery, your own auth. You buy them because the maintenance is endless and the liability is real. A human check is the same shape of decision, with the added weight that the data involved is biometric.

<Note>
  The single biggest reason to buy rather than build is the cross-customer network. On your own, every new account starts as a stranger. On the network, someone already verified with HumanCheck can be recognized on your platform without scanning again, and you still set your own re-verification bar. See [cross-network verification](/learn/cross-network-verification).
</Note>

## The return moment

The decision that surprises most teams is what happens when a verified person comes back, and it is worth getting right before you build anything yourself. You decide how we make sure it is the same person.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Require a fresh check every time">
    Highest assurance. The person does a quick face scan again, and you know with high confidence it is the same human you verified.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Recognize them quietly via a browser identifier">
    No scan at all. We recognize the returning person in the background via a browser identifier, so a real user never has to repeat the check. Note that clearing cookies or switching browsers ends this recognition.
  </Step>
</Steps>

Either way you get real assurance that the person returning is the one you verified, and you tune the bar to the use case. This is the part that is genuinely hard to build well, and it is built in.

## What this costs you to run

Pricing is part of where it fits, so it is worth stating plainly. A verification is about one to two cents, all inclusive, and adding age, gender, or geo costs nothing extra. You are billed monthly on actual usage and charged once per human, so returning people never count again. That is often fifty to a hundred times less than a full KYC check, which is much of why this layer can sit on traffic where a full identity check never could.
